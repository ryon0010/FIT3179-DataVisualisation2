Household Incomes across Malaysia


Household incomes in Malaysia vary across states. In cities like Kuala Lumpur, higher incomes reflect the opportunities of urban life, Meanwhile, rural states like Kelantan tell a different story where traditional livelihoods and limited resources result in lower income levels.

Total number of households in Malaysia in 2022
8,605,100

Despite Selangor having the highest number of households, Kuala Lumpur recorded the highest mean income of RM13,325. This indicates that household numbers do not directly influence income levels, but rather economic activities and opportunities within each state play a more significant role.

Kuala Lumpur's mean household income is 1.7 times higher than the national average, highlighting a significant income disparity. This reflects the city's urbanization and greater economic opportunities

Mean Household Income across States
from 1970 to 2022

Over the years, mean household incomes in Malaysia have shown a steady increase.

However, there was a sudden drop in household income in 2021 for every state, reflecting the economic impact of the Covid-19 pandemic at that time.

Comparison of Mean Household Income and
Expenditure by State

Across states, higher mean household incomes are generally associated with higher expenditures, indicating a proportional relationship where households tend to spend more as their income increases

States with higher incomes, like Kuala Lumpur and Selangor, exhibit this pattern more distinctly, while those with lower incomes, such as Kelantan, face greater constraints on their spending.

Exploring the Relationship Between Household Income, Spending, and Poverty Rates


From the bubble chart, we observe that states with higher mean household incomes, such as Selangor and Kuala Lumpur, exhibit lower spending rates. This trend may indicate that wealthier households allocate a smaller portion of their income toward immediate expenditures, allowing for more savings and investments.


Generally, as spending rates increase, poverty rates tend to rise; however, exceptions exist, such as Sabah, where the spending rate is low, yet the poverty rate remains high. This anomaly could suggest that despite lower expenditures, households in Sabah face significant economic challenges, limiting their income potential and contributing to persistent poverty.
